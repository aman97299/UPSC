The Attorney General for India

The highest law officer in the country, as per Article 76 of the Constitution.

Article 76: Attorney-General for India

1. Appointment & Qualifications

The **President of India** appoints a person to be the Attorney General for India. The person appointed must be qualified to be a Judge of the Supreme Court. The qualifications for a Supreme Court Judge are:

  • They must be a citizen of India.
  • They must have been a Judge of a High Court for at least five years, or an Advocate of a High Court for at least ten years.
  • Alternatively, they must be, in the opinion of the President, a distinguished jurist.

2. Duties

The primary duty of the Attorney General is to give advice to the Government of India on such legal matters as may be referred or assigned to him by the President. They also perform other duties of a legal character and discharge functions conferred on them by the Constitution or any other law.

The Attorney General represents the Government of India in all cases in the Supreme Court and, when required, in any High Court. They also represent the Government in any reference made by the President to the Supreme Court under **Article 143** of the Constitution.

3. Rights and Privileges

In the performance of their duties, the Attorney General has the **right of audience** in all courts throughout India. They also have the right to speak in and participate in the proceedings of both Houses of Parliament, joint sittings of the Houses, and any parliamentary committee of which they may be named a member, but they **do not have the right to vote**.

The Attorney General enjoys all the **privileges and immunities** that are available to a Member of Parliament.

4. Tenure and Remuneration

The Attorney General holds office "**during the pleasure of the President**," which means there is no fixed term. The Constitution does not specify the grounds or procedure for their removal, and the President can remove them at any time. The remuneration is also determined by the President.

Limitations on the Attorney General

While the Attorney General has significant rights, there are certain limitations to ensure there is no conflict of interest:

  • They should not advise or hold a brief against the Government of India.
  • They must not defend accused persons in criminal cases without the permission of the Government.
  • They should not accept an appointment as a director in any company or corporation without Government permission.

It is important to note that the Attorney General is not a full-time counsel for the government and is not barred from private legal practice, though they must abide by the limitations mentioned above.
